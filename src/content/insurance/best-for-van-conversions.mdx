If you own a converted van — or you're about to — you've probably already discovered that insuring it is harder than insuring a regular vehicle. Call your current auto carrier and you'll usually get one of three answers: a confused agent, a flat "we don't do those," or a policy that quietly covers a fraction of what your build is actually worth.

The good news is that the market has changed a lot in the last three years. Roamly exists now. Progressive reversed course on DIY conversions in late 2023. Good Sam's policies have gotten more flexible. State Farm is still a wildcard, but a useful one.

The bad news is that most of the "best insurance for van conversions" articles you'll find online are two to four years old and push you toward whichever company is paying them the highest affiliate commission. This isn't that. Below is an honest, up-to-date comparison of the four carriers that will actually cover a converted van in 2026, what each one costs, and how to pick the right one for your situation.

## Why van conversion insurance is its own category

A standard auto policy covers the vehicle shell and drivetrain. That's it. If you've spent $40,000 turning a Sprinter into a rolling apartment and your van gets totaled, a standard auto policy pays out the cargo van value — maybe $25,000 — and nothing for the build. The cabinetry, solar system, batteries, appliances, flooring, and interior buildout are invisible to the underwriter.

RV or motorhome insurance treats the vehicle as a combined asset: base vehicle plus conversion. When it's done right, the policy covers the full replacement value of everything that's been permanently installed. That's the entire point of insuring a van conversion as an RV instead of as a van.

The catch is that most insurers won't classify a van as an RV unless the conversion meets their requirements. Those requirements vary by carrier, but they usually boil down to the same three things: a permanently installed sleeping area, a permanently installed cooking setup, and documentation proving both exist. Some carriers want more (toilet facilities, water system, photos of the build in progress, receipts). Some want less. The comparison below breaks it down carrier by carrier.

## Roamly

**Best for:** Full-time van lifers, DIY builders, and anyone renting their van on Outdoorsy.

Roamly is the only carrier on this list that was built specifically for converted vans and RVs. It's owned by Outdoorsy (the peer-to-peer RV rental platform), and its entire product exists to solve the insurance problem for people whose rigs don't fit neatly into a traditional auto or RV category. They're licensed as an agency in every state they operate, and they place policies through a panel of underwriters — Spinnaker, Progressive, Safeco, Foremost, National General, and Mobilitas — which lets them match your situation to the carrier most likely to cover it at the best rate.

**What's covered**

Roamly's camper van policy includes the usual liability, comprehensive, and collision, plus:

- **Up to $20,000 in DIY renovation coverage** for custom work and self-installed components. This is the number that matters if you built it yourself.
- **Personal effects coverage up to $5,000** for the gear you keep inside the van.
- **Vacation liability up to $50,000** for when the van is parked and used as temporary accommodation.
- **Emergency expense coverage up to $5,000** for hotel and travel costs after a breakdown or claim.
- **Roadside assistance** with a reported 47-minute average response time.
- **Full-timer package** for people who live in their van.
- **Peer-to-peer rental coverage** — if you rent the van out on Outdoorsy, the policy will cover it while it's being rented. This is rare. Most carriers explicitly exclude rental use.

**Documentation Roamly asks for**

For a DIY build, Roamly typically wants a spreadsheet of materials and costs, photos of major systems during installation (electrical, plumbing), photos of the completed interior and exterior, and receipts for major components (solar, batteries, appliances). For builds over $30,000, they recommend a professional appraisal.

**What it costs**

Roamly publishes rough ranges of $500 to $1,600 per year for most Class B conversions, with Transit builds on the lower end (around $600 to $1,200) and Sprinter builds on the higher end ($800 to $1,600). Actual pricing depends on driving record, garaging state, coverage limits, and whether you bundle with auto insurance.

**The catches**

Roamly doesn't operate in Hawaii or Washington D.C. Their quote process is online-only, which is great for tech-comfortable buyers but frustrating if you want a human to walk you through coverages. And because they're a managing general agency rather than a direct carrier, the underwriter on your actual policy may be one of six different companies, which can make claims a little more complicated than a single-carrier experience.

## Progressive

**Best for:** People who want a name-brand carrier at a low entry price, and who don't need Outdoorsy-specific rental coverage.

Here's where the outdated articles get Progressive wrong. Until late 2023, Progressive was a no for DIY camper van conversions. They insured professionally built rigs but explicitly excluded owner-built ones. That changed in November 2023, when Progressive's RV product manager Matt Jacobs announced the company would begin insuring DIY conversions nationwide, citing the generational shift toward self-built vans as a first entry into RVing.

The rest of the internet hasn't caught up. If you read a "best van insurance" article from 2022 or early 2023, it will tell you Progressive won't cover DIY. That's no longer true.

**What's covered**

Progressive's Class B camper van policy includes:

- **RV comprehensive and collision** for theft, vandalism, fire, weather, and collision damage.
- **Motorhome liability** starting as low as $125 per year, according to Progressive's published rates.
- **Emergency expense** of up to $750 for hotel and travel costs if the van breaks down more than 50 miles from home.
- **Vacation liability** for when the van is parked and used as temporary lodging.
- **Pest damage coverage** (added in the 2023 update).
- **Roof protection coverage** (also added in the 2023 update).
- **Full-timer coverage** for people who live in the van year-round.

**Documentation Progressive asks for**

For a DIY conversion, Progressive wants year, make, and model, a value for the completed build, a list of materials used, intended usage, and photo documentation of upgrades and modifications. They classify the van as a Class B motorhome once it has a kitchen and a permanently installed sleeping area.

**What it costs**

Progressive publishes motorhome liability as starting "as low as $125 per year," though that's the floor for liability-only on the cheapest rigs. A full comprehensive and collision policy on a $60,000 Class B conversion will run closer to the $600 to $1,200 range that's standard across carriers.

**The catches**

Progressive doesn't cover rental income — if you plan to list your van on Outdoorsy or RVshare, Progressive will not cover you during rental use, which can create claim problems if a renter damages the van. Like Roamly, they don't operate in Hawaii or D.C. And some owners report that Progressive's DIY underwriting is stricter than Roamly's for builds over $50,000, meaning you'll need cleaner documentation to get the coverage classified correctly.

## Good Sam Insurance (underwritten by National General)

**Best for:** Traditional RV owners who want a single bundled experience (insurance + roadside + membership + extended warranty).

Good Sam is an insurance agency, not a carrier. The actual policies are underwritten by carriers in the National General Group (now part of Allstate). When you buy a "Good Sam" policy, you're buying a National General policy with Good Sam's service wrapper around it. For most buyers this distinction doesn't matter, but it's worth knowing because it means your claims experience will be with National General's adjusters, not with Good Sam.

**What's covered**

Good Sam's Class B policy includes the standard motorhome coverages — liability, comprehensive, collision, personal belongings, roadside assistance — plus several features worth knowing about:

- **Total loss replacement** for newer RVs. If your van is totaled within the first five model years, the policy will pay to replace it with a new, equivalent van rather than just cutting a check for depreciated value. This is one of the more generous total-loss features in the market.
- **Permanent attachments coverage**. Awnings, antennas, satellite dishes, and similar exterior attachments are automatically covered without needing to be scheduled separately.
- **Suspendable coverage for storage periods**. If your van sits unused for part of the year, you can drop most of the coverage during storage and only pay for comprehensive. This is useful for seasonal users.
- **Full-timer package** for people living in the van full-time.
- **Bundle discounts with Good Sam Roadside Assistance and Good Sam Club membership**, which can average around $349 per year in savings according to their published rates.

**Documentation Good Sam asks for**

For Class B camper vans — including DIY conversions — Good Sam requires permanently installed cooking and sleeping facilities and exterior/interior photos upfront to show the conversion is real and meets their standards. They do not require professional certification (no RVIA seal), but the build has to look built.

**What it costs**

Good Sam doesn't publish a base rate the way Progressive does, but their countrywide averages for combined travel trailer and motorhome policies are in line with the broader market. Real-world pricing for a Class B conversion typically lands in the same $600 to $1,500 range as Roamly and Progressive.

**The catches**

Good Sam doesn't offer rental income coverage. The Good Sam experience is heavily tied to the broader Good Sam ecosystem (club membership, roadside, extended warranty, campground discounts) — which is great if you want all of that and awkward if you don't. And because the underwriter is National General, claim experiences vary depending on which regional adjusters handle your case.

## State Farm

**Best for:** People who already have State Farm auto insurance and want the discount, and who are willing to shop for a cooperative local agent.

State Farm is the wildcard. On paper, State Farm does not have a specific DIY conversion product — they don't advertise Class B camper van coverage, and their corporate materials don't mention van conversions at all. In practice, plenty of converted van owners are insured through State Farm, often at prices significantly lower than the specialty carriers.

Here's how it actually works: State Farm is structured around local agents, and underwriting authority for non-standard vehicles often sits with the agent rather than corporate. One agent will tell you flat-out "we don't insure those." The agent in the next office will write the policy same-day after looking at photos. It's not consistent across the country, and it's not even consistent within the same state.

**What owners are actually reporting**

Real examples from forums and owner reports:

- A Sprinter conversion insured by State Farm as a Class B camper for **$414 per year** with comprehensive coverage, requiring only photos of the interior and exterior.
- A Colorado Sprinter conversion with full coverage, towing, and a $1,000 deductible at **$59.97 per month** (about $720 per year).
- Multiple reports of agents refusing to quote after requesting photos and cost breakdowns.

The pattern is clear: when State Farm says yes, they tend to be the cheapest option on the market. When they say no, they just say no.

**What to do if you want to try State Farm**

Two things help. First, call a local agent directly rather than the corporate line — corporate will usually route you to an online quote form that rejects non-standard vehicles. Second, have your van retitled as a housecar, motorhome, or RV with your state DMV before you call. Agents have more underwriting flexibility when the registration already shows the vehicle as a motorhome. Several owners have reported that an initial State Farm rejection became an approval after they retitled the van and called back.

**The catches**

State Farm doesn't publicly document any DIY van coverage, so what you get is whatever your agent writes. That means inconsistent terms, no guarantee of renewal, and no standard "build coverage" the way Roamly or Good Sam publish it. Full-timer coverage is hit or miss. And if you move or change agents, the new agent may not honor the original arrangement.

## Who will not cover you

A few carriers that come up in searches are worth mentioning only so you can cross them off your list:

- **AAA** will not insure DIY van conversions in California as of recent owner reports. Other states vary, but California is a hard no.
- **Foremost, Safeco, and Mobilitas** write van conversion policies, but only through Roamly's panel — you can't buy them direct.
- **USAA** will insure converted vans for military members and their families, but membership is restricted. If you're eligible, USAA is often the cheapest option. If you're not, there's no path in.
- **GEICO, Allstate, Farmers, Liberty Mutual, and Nationwide** do not have published DIY camper van products. Individual agents may write something, but there's no standardized coverage to count on.

## How to choose

Pick your carrier based on your situation, not on whichever article you read first:

**You built it yourself and the build is under $50,000.**
Start with Progressive. It's the cheapest of the name-brand carriers, their DIY product is now explicitly available, and their documentation requirements are reasonable. If they won't classify your build as a Class B, move to Roamly.

**You built it yourself and the build is over $50,000.**
Start with Roamly. Their $20,000 DIY renovation coverage and panel-of-carriers model are built for higher-value custom builds, and their underwriting is more flexible for complex electrical and plumbing systems. Progressive may still be cheaper, but Roamly will usually place the policy faster.

**You bought a professionally built van.**
Any of the four will cover you, and the decision comes down to price and service. Get quotes from all four. Good Sam's total loss replacement is the best feature if your van is less than five model years old. Progressive is usually cheapest. Roamly is the easiest online experience.

**You're full-time in the van.**
Roamly and Good Sam both offer real full-timer packages designed for this. Progressive offers full-timer coverage but with more restrictions. State Farm is inconsistent. Start with Roamly if you also want rental income coverage; start with Good Sam if you want the bundled roadside and club membership.

**You plan to rent the van on Outdoorsy.**
Roamly. This is the only carrier on the list that covers peer-to-peer rental use, and it's the entire reason Roamly exists. Every other carrier will treat rental use as an uncovered commercial activity.

**You already have State Farm auto insurance and want the cheapest option possible.**
Call your local State Farm agent, ask if they'll write a Class B camper van policy, and have your retitled registration and build photos ready. If they say yes, it'll likely be the cheapest quote you get. If they say no, move on.

## What to document regardless of who you pick

Every carrier in the comparison above will ask for some version of the same documentation package. Prepare it once and you can shop all four quotes in an afternoon:

1. **A spreadsheet of build costs.** Every major component, what you paid, where you bought it. Include labor if you hired anyone for any portion. This becomes the basis for the insured value.
2. **Photos of the completed build.** Interior from every angle, exterior, the kitchen area clearly showing the cooktop or stove, the sleeping area clearly showing a permanent bed or convertible dinette, any bathroom or toilet area.
3. **Photos of major systems during installation** if you have them. Electrical before the walls went on, plumbing before flooring, insulation and framing. These matter more for higher-value builds.
4. **Receipts for major components.** Solar panels, batteries, inverter, refrigerator, cooktop, water heater, any appliance over $500.
5. **Current registration.** If you've already retitled the van as a housecar, motorhome, or RV, bring that. Carriers classify builds faster when the DMV has already classified them.
6. **A professional appraisal** if your total build value is over $30,000. Roamly specifically recommends this. Good Sam and Progressive will sometimes require one for higher-value claims.

## What to ask when you call

Five questions that separate a good quote from a bad one:

1. **"Is this being written as a Class B motorhome policy or as an auto policy?"** An auto policy won't cover the build. Never accept an auto policy on a converted van.
2. **"Does the coverage amount include the conversion value, or just the base vehicle?"** The answer should include the conversion. If the quoted coverage is only the cargo van value, the policy will underpay a total loss by tens of thousands of dollars.
3. **"Is this agreed value or actual cash value?"** Agreed value pays out the insured amount in a total loss. Actual cash value pays out depreciated value, which is usually less. Agreed value is what you want for a custom build.
4. **"Does the policy cover my build during the remaining construction?"** If your van isn't finished, ask explicitly about coverage during the rest of the build. Most RV policies won't cover a van that isn't yet legally classified as a motorhome.
5. **"What happens to this policy if I rent the van out on Outdoorsy or RVshare?"** The answer from Roamly is "still covered." The answer from every other carrier is "coverage suspended." Know which one you're getting.

## Bottom line

In 2026 there are four real choices for insuring a converted van in the United States: Roamly, Progressive, Good Sam, and State Farm (if you can find a willing agent). Roamly is the specialist and covers the edge cases. Progressive is the name-brand carrier that finally covers DIY. Good Sam is the bundled RV-ecosystem option with the best total-loss replacement feature. State Farm is the wildcard with the lowest potential price.

Most van conversion owners will get the best result by quoting at least Roamly and Progressive side-by-side, adding Good Sam if they want the total loss replacement feature, and trying State Farm last if they already have a relationship with an agent who's willing to write it. Skip the carriers that don't have a documented DIY product — a policy that isn't clearly written for your vehicle type is a policy that will fight you at claim time.

Insurance is the part of van ownership nobody thinks about until something goes wrong. Spend one afternoon getting quotes from all four, pick the one that fits your actual use case, and document everything. That's the whole playbook.
