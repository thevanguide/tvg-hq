The Ford Transit sits in the middle of the van conversion market — more affordable than the Sprinter, better driving dynamics than the ProMaster, and available with AWD from the factory. It is also one of the most common commercial vehicles on US roads, which means a deep parts network, a wide service network, and insurers that understand the platform well.

For van conversion owners, the Transit's insurance story is straightforward. It does not carry the Sprinter's premium-parts cost penalty, and it does not have the ProMaster's thinner service network in some regions. The areas where Transit insurance gets specific — the AWD option, the EcoBoost engine, and the cargo vs. passenger van distinction — are covered below.

## What Makes the Transit Different for Insurance

**Ford parts availability and cost.** Ford's parts and service infrastructure is the largest of the three major van platforms in the US. Replacement parts are widely available, and the network of qualified Ford service shops is dense in almost every market. For insurers, this means lower expected claim costs on repairs relative to the Sprinter. Premiums reflect that.

**Gas engine with no diesel complications.** The Transit is available with a 3.5L V6 (standard), 3.5L EcoBoost V6 (turbocharged), and a 2.0L EcoBoost in newer model years. All are gas engines. No DEF system, no diesel particulate filter, no emissions-related complications. This simplifies the maintenance and repair cost profile that insurers use to price coverage.

**Factory AWD option.** The Transit is the only full-size van platform available with factory AWD in the US (since the 2020 model year). AWD Transits are popular in the conversion market for their capability in mountain and winter conditions. From an insurance standpoint, AWD adds a modest cost increment — AWD drivetrains are more expensive to repair than RWD — but some carriers may view AWD as a risk reduction factor in slippery conditions. The net effect on premiums is typically small.

**Cargo van vs. passenger van VIN.** Ford sells the Transit in both cargo van and passenger van configurations. If you bought a passenger Transit (with factory rear windows and seating) and converted it, the VIN already reflects a passenger vehicle. If you bought a cargo Transit, the VIN reflects a commercial vehicle. Some carriers treat these differently at the quoting stage. Retitling to housecar or motorhome resolves the distinction, but it is worth knowing which VIN you have when you start calling carriers.

## What Transit Insurance Costs

| Coverage Type | Typical Annual Range |
|---|---|
| Class B / campervan policy (converted Transit) | $450–$1,400 |
| Standard personal auto (passenger Transit) | $600–$1,400 |

Transit insurance costs are comparable to ProMaster and modestly below Sprinter for the same coverage on the same build value. The Ford parts advantage and gas engine keep repair cost assumptions lower than the Sprinter's diesel drivetrain.

The EcoBoost engine may add a small premium increment over the base V6 due to the turbocharger repair cost. In practice, this is a minor factor relative to the build value, use classification, and driver profile.

## Which Carriers Cover Transit Conversions

Every carrier in the campervan insurance market covers the Transit. The platform's domestic origin, gas engine, and high sales volume make it one of the easiest vans to underwrite.

**Roamly.** Covers DIY and custom Transit conversions. No platform-specific restrictions. Transit's broad parts availability and straightforward VIN decoding make it a clean underwriting target across Roamly's carrier panel.

**Progressive.** Covers DIY Transit conversions (since November 2023) and professional builds under the RV product. Transits quote well in most states.

**Good Sam / National General.** Full coverage options available, including full replacement cost, personal belongings, and full-timer's. No Transit-specific exclusions.

**State Farm.** Agent-driven. Writes converted Transits, including DIY, with standard documentation requirements.

**USAA.** Military families only. Covers converted Transits at competitive rates.

For a detailed carrier comparison, see [Best Insurance for Van Conversions](/insurance/best-for-van-conversions/).

## Transit-Specific Considerations

**The 150/250/350 question.** The Transit 150, 250, and 350 differ in payload capacity and GVWR, not in body size (within the same roof height and wheelbase). The 250 and 350 High Roof models with the 148" extended wheelbase are the most common conversion platforms. Insurance does not vary significantly between these unless the 350 is a cutaway or cab chassis variant, which some carriers classify differently from the standard cargo van body.

**EcoBoost engine and turbocharger repair costs.** The 3.5L EcoBoost V6 is a popular choice for the extra towing capability and highway power. The turbocharger system adds a repair cost variable — turbo replacements run $1,500 to $3,000+ — that the naturally aspirated V6 does not have. This is a minor factor in insurance pricing but worth noting for total cost of ownership.

**AWD drivetrain repair costs.** The AWD Transit's drivetrain is more complex (and more expensive to repair) than the RWD version. In a significant accident affecting the drivetrain, the repair bill will be higher. Carriers price this in, but the premium difference between AWD and RWD Transits is typically modest — in the range of 3 to 8 percent, often less than the impact of a single accident on the driver's record.

**Retitling applies here too.** A cargo Transit on commercial plates gets quoted as a commercial vehicle. Retitle to housecar or motorhome before shopping for campervan insurance. The process varies by state — see the [California registration guide](/registration/california/) for one example.

## Documentation to Have Ready

Before calling any carrier for a Transit conversion quote:

- Registration document showing current title status
- VIN and base vehicle specs (model year, 150/250/350, wheelbase, roof height, engine, RWD/AWD)
- Build value broken into base vehicle + conversion cost
- Build documentation: component receipts, completed interior photos
- Intended use: recreational, full-time, any business use
- Storage location and annual mileage estimate
