TJ Rivers, owner of [Venturhaus Vans](/builders/colorado/venturhaus-vans/) in Evergreen, Colorado, put it bluntly in an email on April 16, 2026: *"NOAH had a list of lenders on their website, most of these only worked with [tiny homes on wheels], the one that worked with vans got bought and no longer financed vans. Could not find a lender that accepted NOAH. No, we didn't end up with any lender financing a NOAH build for us. Yes — NOAH definitely presented financing access as part of the value, so I felt pretty deceived when I went to access their 'lenders' and they only lended on THOW."*

NOAH Certified markets financing as one of the four headline benefits of certification. Whether that benefit actually materializes for a van conversion buyer in April 2026 is a separate question — and the answer, pieced together from NOAH's own public marketing, NOAH's own member-facing agreements, the published eligibility rules of the major RV lenders, and the experience of at least one builder who pursued it: **the gap between the marketing and the delivered value is large, and it matters for anyone considering NOAH certification specifically to help with financing a van conversion.**

This article walks through exactly what NOAH says in its marketing, exactly what NOAH says in the agreements members sign, exactly which lenders NOAH currently lists, and exactly what the major RV lenders commonly cited for van conversions require. All primary sources are linked inline so you can verify any of it yourself.

## What NOAH Promotes Publicly

NOAH's homepage at [noahcertified.org](https://noahcertified.org/) leads with this headline above the hero image:

> "Improve Your Tiny Home's Placement, Insurance, Financing & Salability"

Financing is one of four headline benefits. The homepage also features testimonials that make concrete claims about financing access. From Mitchcraft Tiny Homes:

> "Being NOAH Certified allows our clients to have access to better financing options and lower insurance rates too!"

From Maverick Tiny Homes:

> "They inspect to all National Building, Plumbing, Electric, and RV codes; which helps with zoning, insurance, and even financing your tiny home!"

These are builder testimonials hosted on NOAH's own marketing site, not disclosed partnerships with specific lenders.

NOAH's main navigation has a "Resources" link. The [Resources page](https://noahcertified.org/associates/) is split into three tabs: **Financing | Insurance | Associates**. As of April 16, 2026, the entire **Financing** tab lists exactly one institution.

## The Single Lender on NOAH's Financing Tab

The institution NOAH lists under Financing is **Frost Bank**, with this product description published verbatim on the Resources page:

> "Finance a home improvement project. We offer two types of Frost Home Improvement Loans — one that uses your home as collateral, and one that doesn't. No matter which loan type is right for you, our lending team has an extensive vendor network and can refer you to trusted contractors, builders and more."

Frost Bank's Home Improvement Loan is not an RV loan. It is a personal loan or home-equity product for homeowners funding improvements to a residence they already own. It has nothing to do with financing a van conversion as a vehicle. For a homeowner with equity, it is identical to any other home-improvement loan they could get without ever hearing the word "NOAH." For a non-homeowner, it is not available at all.

This is the complete list of financing partners NOAH currently publishes.

## What NOAH Discloses to Members Behind the Sign-Up

Once a builder or buyer commits to NOAH and reviews the [Builder Membership Pledge](https://noahcertified.org/builder-pledge/) — the onboarding contract every NOAH member signs — the framing changes. The Pledge contains this sentence, which appears **four separate times** in the same document:

> "NOAH Inspection and Certification does not guarantee Insurance, Financing or Placement."

The same disclaimer language appears on two other member-facing pages: the [Member Manual](https://noahcertified.org/member-manual/) and the [VC Onboarding Form](https://noahcertified.org/vc-onboarding-form/).

It does not appear on NOAH's homepage, FAQ page, Why NOAH page, Become a NOAH Builder page, or the Resources page where financing is actually marketed. A prospective builder or buyer evaluating whether NOAH certification is worth paying for would not encounter this disclaimer in the course of the decision-making pages — only after the commitment to join.

NOAH is not legally obligated to put the disclaimer on every marketing page. The disclaimer exists on a live public URL. The observation is narrower than that: the material in the sign-up contract is meaningfully more hedged than the material in the marketing, and a reasonable prospective buyer weighing the financing benefit would not see the hedged version without signing up first.

## What RV Lenders Actually Say About NOAH

To check whether NOAH certification does in fact unlock RV financing in practice, we reviewed the published eligibility language of the major RV lenders most commonly cited for van conversions. **None of them names NOAH in its eligibility rules.** Several explicitly exclude vehicle categories that would cover most NOAH-certified van conversions.

**Good Sam Finance Center** ([loans.goodsam.com/faqs](https://loans.goodsam.com/faqs)) finances "Class A, B, C & Super C Motorhomes, Class B/C Conversions, Toter-homes, Overland/Adventure RVs (i.e. Earth Roamer, TouRig, Earth Cruiser, etc), Bus Conversions" and explicitly excludes "Full-timers, Park Models, Tiny Homes, or Rental Units." NOAH is not mentioned. Eligibility is based on vehicle category and NADA Guides listing, not on any third-party inspection or certification.

**Southeast Financial** ([sefinancial.com/faq/loans/](https://www.sefinancial.com/faq/loans/)) states, verbatim: *"We do not offer financing for the following types of vehicles: destination trailers, conversion vans, park models, toter homes, ATV's, and house boats."* Conversion vans are categorically excluded regardless of certification.

**Essex Credit / Bank of the West / BMO** ([essexcredit.com/home/learn/rv-faqs/](https://www.essexcredit.com/home/learn/rv-faqs/)) will not finance "tiny homes, park models, or toterhomes." No mention of NOAH or any third-party certification program.

**Medallion Bank** ([medallionbank.com](https://www.medallionbank.com/)) finances new and used RVs "listed in the JD Power Appraisal Guide." NOAH is not mentioned anywhere in their public eligibility materials.

**My Financing USA** ([myfinancingusa.com/rv-loans/](https://www.myfinancingusa.com/rv-loans/)) describes itself as "a contributing and accredited member of the RVIA" — the competing certification program — but that is the company's own membership, not a borrower requirement. Their published eligibility lists a credit-score minimum and excludes "Park Models or Schoolies." NOAH is not mentioned.

The absence is the finding. Where van conversions do successfully get financed as RVs, the unlock is not NOAH. It is typically one of three things: (1) buying from a builder whose units meet the lender's vehicle-category definition and appear in NADA or JD Power, (2) meeting the destination state's DMV definition of an RV (sleeping, cooking, water, and storage provisions present at inspection), or (3) routing through a specialty or unsecured product that is indifferent to certification altogether.

## Why This Gap Exists: NOAH Is Built for Tiny Homes, Not Vans

NOAH's product focus, documented across their [FAQ](https://noahcertified.org/faq/) and [Why NOAH](https://noahcertified.org/why-noah/) pages, is tiny homes on wheels (THOW) and stationary tiny homes. The FAQ questions are about parking, zoning, framing, DIY trailers, tempered glass for transit, and ANSI 119.5 compliance. Van conversions are adjacent to their core product — some of the same safety standards apply — but they are not the center of NOAH's program.

This matters because the lender conversations NOAH has historically had are tiny-home conversations. The one active financing partner they currently publish, Frost Bank, offers home-improvement loans — a product that fits a buyer placing a tiny home on their own land far better than a buyer financing a Class B van conversion as a vehicle.

TJ Rivers' description in the April 2026 email matches this pattern exactly:

> "NOAH had a list of lenders on their website, most of these only worked with THOW, the one that worked with vans got bought and no longer financed vans."

## What to Do If You Want RV Financing on a Custom Van

If you are a van buyer specifically interested in RV-loan financing — lower rates, longer terms, secured against the vehicle — the practical paths that actually work as of April 2026 are:

1. **Buy from an RVIA-certified builder.** This is the single strongest unlock for most RV lender programs. See [Van Conversion Certification: What the Seals Mean](/certification/) for which builders carry RVIA membership and why it matters more for financing than NOAH does.

2. **Register the van as an RV first, then refinance into an RV loan.** Oregon, Texas, Montana, and several other states will reclassify a converted van as a motorhome at DMV inspection based on physical features — a permanently installed bed, cooking capability, water system, and storage. Once titled as a motorhome, some lenders (including credit unions like Umpqua in the Pacific Northwest) will refinance an auto loan or line of credit into an RV loan. See our [state-by-state registration guides](/registration/) for the specific definitions that apply.

3. **Use personal-loan products designed to work around certification entirely.** [LightStream](https://www.lightstream.com/), [SoFi](https://www.sofi.com/personal-loans/), and home-equity products fund van conversions without any certification requirement. Our [How to Finance a Van Conversion Without RVIA Certification](/financing/without-rvia/) guide walks through the options in detail.

None of these three paths require or are improved by NOAH certification.

## What to Ask NOAH Before You Pay

If you are still considering NOAH certification and financing access is part of why — it is reasonable to ask NOAH, in writing, the following questions before paying any certification fee:

1. Which specific lenders have, in the past twelve months, approved an RV loan on a NOAH-certified Class B van conversion? Can NOAH provide contact information for the underwriter who approved the loan, or for a customer who received one?
2. Does the lender listed on the NOAH Resources / Financing tab (currently Frost Bank) have an RV-specific loan product for NOAH-certified van conversions, or is the referral for their Home Improvement Loan only?
3. If NOAH's position is that the certification does not guarantee financing — as stated four times in the Builder Membership Pledge — does NOAH publicly disclose this on the marketing pages where the financing benefit is advertised?

A direct written response to these three questions is the minimum due diligence a reasonable buyer should expect before spending on certification that is being purchased partly for financing access.

## The Bottom Line

NOAH Certified's public marketing presents financing access as a benefit of certification. NOAH's own member-facing agreements state, four separate times, that the certification does not guarantee financing. The single lender currently listed on NOAH's Resources page offers a home-improvement loan, not an RV loan. None of the major RV lenders surveyed mentions NOAH in its public eligibility language. A builder who pursued NOAH specifically for the financing benefit reported being unable to find any lender who would fund a NOAH-certified van conversion.

Every individual marketing claim NOAH makes is technically defensible. The testimonials are third-party. The lender listed is a real institution. The headline "improves financing" can be read generously — a referral to any lender is technically an improvement over no referral. What is harder to defend is the gap between what the total marketing package implies and what the certification actually delivers for a van conversion buyer: the practical answer to "will NOAH help me finance my custom van build through an RV loan" is, in April 2026, no.

If a buyer wants their custom van conversion financed as a recreational vehicle, the practical paths are RVIA-member builders, state DMV reclassification as a motorhome, or personal-loan products designed to bypass certification requirements altogether. NOAH is not the path.
