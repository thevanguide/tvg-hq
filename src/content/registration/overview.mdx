If you converted a cargo van into a camper and left it titled as a commercial vehicle, you are carrying three problems at once: your insurance does not cover the build, you are paying weight fees you no longer owe, and the vehicle's legal classification does not match what it actually is. Fixing this requires retitling the van as an RV, housecar, motorhome, or camper — the terminology varies by state — and every state runs the process slightly differently.

This page covers what the process looks like in general, which parts are the same everywhere, which parts are where states diverge, and where to find the detailed guide for the state you are registering in.

## What Retitling Actually Means

Retitling is a DMV process that changes a vehicle's classification from one body type to another — in this case, from a commercial cargo van to a recreational vehicle type (the specific name varies by state: "housecar" in California, "motor home" in Florida, "RV" in Vermont, and so on). The change affects:

- **Legal classification** — the vehicle is now recognized as a recreational vehicle under the state's vehicle code
- **Registration fees and weight fees** — commercial weight fees typically drop away
- **Insurance eligibility** — most campervan and RV insurance products require RV-class titling
- **Emissions rules** (sometimes) — depends on state and vehicle GVWR
- **Parking and use rules** (sometimes) — affects things like overnight parking and commercial vehicle restrictions

It does not typically change who owns the vehicle, the VIN, or the underlying title history. It is a reclassification of an existing title, not a new title.

## What Every State Has in Common

Regardless of which state you are registering in, every van-to-RV retitling process shares a core structure:

1. **Minimum habitation features.** Every state requires the van to have some combination of permanent sleeping, cooking, water, toilet, and storage facilities. The specific list varies, but the principle — "the van must actually be equipped for living in" — is universal.
2. **Physical inspection.** Most states require a physical inspection of the vehicle by a DMV or authorized inspector. A few states (Vermont, most notably) allow remote or mail-in processes.
3. **Documentation.** Every state requires the current title, current registration, and a form certifying the body type change or human habitation status.
4. **A body type change in the state's vehicle record.** The vehicle's registered body type (often a code like "VN" for cargo van) is changed to an RV-class code.
5. **Updated registration and title.** You receive new registration at the appointment and a new title document by mail a few weeks later.

The entire process is typically completable in a single DMV appointment, with the new title arriving separately.

## Where States Diverge

The differences between states are significant enough that it pays to read the specific guide for your state before attempting the process.

**Inspection strictness.** California inspectors look for permanent, visible habitation features and will deny builds that still look like cargo vans. New York is stricter. Vermont does not inspect at all for the mail-in process. Florida accepts an owner affidavit.

**Equipment requirements.** California requires a toilet, cooking facility, storage, sleeping area, and water source for van camper classification. Florida requires only one of a shorter list of features for owner-converted vehicles. Oregon requires a bed and cooking facilities. The minimum varies substantially.

**Fees and taxes.** Registration fees, title fees, and recurring Vehicle License Fees or ad valorem taxes vary by state. California's VLF is calculated on vehicle value and can increase substantially if conversion costs are captured on the paperwork. Other states use flat fees or different calculation methods.

**Residency requirements.** Most states require you to be a resident to register a vehicle there. Vermont famously does not, which is why it is commonly used by out-of-staters who want a simpler registration path. Montana's LLC registration strategy uses a corporate workaround in a similar way. Both carry tax and legal implications worth understanding before using them.

**Commercial-to-RV plate swap.** Some states require you to surrender commercial plates and receive automotive or RV plates as part of the reclassification. Others simply update the body type code without changing the plates.

**Smog and emissions.** Retitling usually does not change a state's emissions rules, which are based on fuel type, model year, and gross vehicle weight rating. But some states tie inspection schedules to vehicle class, which means reclassifying can shift when you are due for a smog check.

## Why This Matters for Insurance

Most campervan insurance carriers — Roamly, Good Sam / National General, Progressive, State Farm — require the vehicle to be titled as a recreational vehicle, housecar, motorhome, or camper before they will write an RV-class policy on it. If the van is still on commercial plates, you are stuck with commercial auto insurance that usually does not cover the conversion value.

Retitling is the step that unlocks the full campervan insurance market. See [Camper Van Insurance](/insurance/) for an overview of what the product covers and which carriers to consider once the retitling is done.

## A Note on Montana LLC and Vermont Registration

Two non-resident registration strategies come up regularly enough to address directly.

**Montana LLC registration** involves forming a Montana LLC and registering the vehicle to the LLC, which avoids sales tax and provides other potential benefits. It is legal if done correctly, but several states — including California — have aggressively pursued residents who use Montana LLCs to avoid their home state's taxes, and penalties can be substantial. Understand your home state's rules before considering this approach.

**Vermont registration** allows non-residents to title a vehicle in Vermont via a mail-in process with no inspection. It is popular with van owners who want a simpler registration path. Like Montana LLC, it carries tax implications and does not exempt you from your home state's residency and registration rules. It is best treated as a tool, not a shortcut.
