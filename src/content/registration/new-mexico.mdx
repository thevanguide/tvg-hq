New Mexico handles van conversion registration through its Motor Vehicle Division (MVD), not a "DMV" like most states. The state has a well-defined process for specially constructed and reconstructed vehicles that applies to van conversions seeking a motor home or recreational vehicle classification. Two specific forms, the MVD10015 and MVD10053, are the cornerstones of that process.

New Mexico is also notable for its emissions testing requirements, which apply only in Bernalillo County (the Albuquerque metro area) and include a specific exemption for motorhomes. If your converted van is titled as a motorhome, it is exempt from emissions testing even in Bernalillo County.

The state gives you 90 days from purchase to title a vehicle before penalty taxes kick in. Miss that window, and the motor vehicle excise tax increases from 4% to 6%. Here is the full process.

## What New Mexico Calls Your Van

New Mexico uses the term **motorized home** in its vehicle classification system. The MVD's [Vehicle Definitions page](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/vehicles/vehicle-definitions/) and [Vehicle Procedures Manual](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/resources/mvd-procedures-manuals/vehicle-procedures-manual/) define the categories of recreational vehicles including motor homes, van conversions, travel trailers, truck campers, and chassis-mount campers.

The [MVD's Chapter 15 on Recreational Vehicles](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/chapter-15-recreational-vehicles/) governs the titling and registration of all RV types, including converted vans. A van conversion falls under the broader recreational vehicle umbrella and can be titled with a motor home body type if the conversion meets the requirements.

For registration purposes, the distinction between a "van" and a "motorized home" determines plate type, insurance eligibility, and emissions testing exemption status.

## What Your Van Needs to Qualify

New Mexico requires that a vehicle classified as a motorized home or recreational vehicle be designed or modified to provide temporary living quarters for recreational, camping, or travel use. The state does not publish a specific four-of-six or four-of-seven feature checklist like some states.

When applying for a body type change through the specially constructed or reconstructed vehicle process, the MVD evaluates your submission based on the forms and documentation you provide. The [Vehicle Equipment Affirmation (Form MVD10053)](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/forms/most-popular-forms/) requires you to affirm that the vehicle's equipment meets New Mexico standards, including federal highway safety standards.

Features that demonstrate a legitimate living-quarters conversion include:

- A permanent sleeping area
- Cooking facilities (stove, cooktop, or built-in appliance)
- A water system with a sink and faucet
- A toilet (portable or plumbed)
- A refrigerator or ice box
- Climate control (heater, air conditioning)
- A 12V or 120V electrical system
- Built-in storage and cabinetry

The conversion should be permanent and clearly intended for habitation, not loose camping equipment placed inside a cargo area.

## The Registration Process, Step by Step

### Step 1: Complete the Conversion

Finish the build so the van is clearly configured as a living space with permanent habitation features installed.

### Step 2: Complete the Required MVD Forms

Two forms are central to the process:

**[Affirmation for Specially Constructed or Reconstructed Vehicle (MVD10015)](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/wp-content/uploads/2020/12/mvd10015.pdf):** This form requires you to attest that the vehicle has been constructed or reconstructed. You describe the vehicle, its components, and the nature of the conversion.

**[Vehicle Equipment Affirmation (MVD10053)](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/forms/most-popular-forms/):** This form verifies that the vehicle's equipment meets New Mexico standards for titling, including compliance with federal standards for highway use.

Both forms must be completed and signed by the applicant.

### Step 3: Obtain a Weight Certificate

New Mexico requires a **weight certificate** for specially constructed or reconstructed vehicles. You can obtain one at a certified scale (truck stops, recycling centers, and some auto shops have public scales). The weight certificate documents the vehicle's actual weight after conversion.

### Step 4: Gather All Supporting Documents

Collect the following:

- Completed MVD10015 (Affirmation for Specially Constructed or Reconstructed Vehicle)
- Completed MVD10053 (Vehicle Equipment Affirmation)
- Weight certificate
- Current vehicle title (NM or out-of-state)
- Bills of sale or invoices for all parts used in the conversion
- Manufacturer's Certificate of Origin (if applicable)
- Valid identification
- Proof of New Mexico liability insurance
- Payment for title fee, registration, and motor vehicle excise tax

### Step 5: Visit an MVD Field Office or Authorized Provider

New Mexico allows you to handle titling and registration at [MVD field offices](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/vehicles/vehicle-registration/register-your-vehicle/) or through authorized private MVD service providers (such as [MVD Now](https://www.mvdnow.com/) or [In & Out MVD](https://inandoutmvd.com/)). Private providers charge a service fee on top of the state fees but often have shorter wait times and more convenient hours.

Bring all documents to the office. The MVD will review the forms and, if approved, issue a title with the motor home body type and register the vehicle.

### Step 6: Receive Title, Plates, and Registration

New Mexico issues registration and plates at the time of the transaction. Your title will either be issued on the spot or mailed to you, depending on the office and the complexity of the transaction.

## Fees

New Mexico's fee structure includes a motor vehicle excise tax, title fee, and registration fees. The amounts below reflect published rates as of early 2026.

| Fee | Amount | Source |
|-----|--------|--------|
| Motor Vehicle Excise Tax (MVET) | 4% of purchase price | [NM Tax & Rev](https://www.tax.newmexico.gov/all-nm-taxes/motor-vehicle-taxes-fees/) |
| Title fee | $5 | [MVD Fees](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/chapter-6-fees-penalties-and-excise-tax/) |
| Registration fee (RV/motorhome) | Varies by weight and type | [MVD Fees](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/what-is-the-fee-for-registering-my-vehicle/) |
| License plates | Included in registration | [MVD](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/vehicles/vehicle-registration/) |
| Late title penalty (after 90 days) | MVET increases to 6% | [MVD Chapter 6](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/chapter-6-fees-penalties-and-excise-tax/) |
| Private MVD provider service fee | $15–$40 | Provider-specific |

### Motor Vehicle Excise Tax

The MVET is New Mexico's equivalent of a sales tax on vehicles. It is **4% of the purchase price**, less any trade-in credit. This tax is collected at the time of titling.

If you do not title the vehicle within **90 days of purchase**, the tax rate increases from 4% to 6% as a penalty. This is a significant financial incentive to title promptly.

The MVET is based on the purchase price of the vehicle, not the value of the completed conversion. If you purchased a used van for $25,000, the MVET is calculated on that $25,000, not on the $25,000 plus whatever you spent on the conversion.

### Registration Fees

Registration fees for RVs and motorhomes vary based on vehicle weight and type. Contact the [MVD](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/what-is-the-fee-for-registering-my-vehicle/) or use a private provider's [fee calculator](https://inandoutmvd.com/mvd-title-registration-calculator/) for a specific estimate based on your vehicle.

### Timelines

- **All vehicle purchases**: Title within [90 days](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/chapter-6-fees-penalties-and-excise-tax/) to avoid the penalty excise tax increase from 4% to 6%.
- **New New Mexico residents**: Register your out-of-state vehicle within 30 days of establishing residency.
- Registration must be renewed annually.

## Emissions Testing

New Mexico's emissions testing program is limited to **Bernalillo County** (Albuquerque metro area). If your van is registered outside Bernalillo County, no emissions testing is required.

Within Bernalillo County, per the [MVD emissions testing page](https://www.mvd.newmexico.gov/vehicles/emission-testing/):

- **Motorhomes are exempt** from emissions testing requirements
- Diesel vehicles are exempt
- Electric vehicles are exempt
- Vehicles with a GVWR exceeding 10,001 lbs are exempt
- Vehicles model year 1975 and older are exempt
- New vehicles are exempt for the first four model years

Residents of Bernalillo County, as well as those who commute into Bernalillo County 60 or more days per year, are required to have their vehicles tested, unless an exemption applies.

The motorhome exemption is a significant practical benefit of retitling your van. If you live in the Albuquerque area, a van titled as a "van" would need emissions testing, but the same van titled as a "motorhome" would not.

## Insurance After Registration

Once your van is titled as a motorized home, you become eligible for RV and campervan insurance policies that cover the full build value. New Mexico requires all registered vehicles to carry minimum liability insurance.

See [Best Insurance for Van Conversions](/insurance/best-for-van-conversions/) for a comparison of carriers that write policies on converted vans, including which ones require a motor home title and which will insure builds on a standard auto policy.

## Common Pitfalls

**Missing the 90-day title deadline.** New Mexico's penalty for late titling is substantial: the motor vehicle excise tax increases from 4% to 6% of the purchase price. On a $30,000 van, that is an extra $600. Title promptly.

**Not having parts receipts.** The MVD requires bills of sale or invoices for all parts used in the conversion. If you built the conversion over time and discarded receipts along the way, reconstruct as much documentation as you can. Missing receipts can delay or prevent approval.

**Forgetting the weight certificate.** This is a required document for specially constructed or reconstructed vehicles. Obtain one at a certified scale before visiting the MVD. Showing up without it means a return trip.

**Going to an MVD field office without an appointment.** MVD field offices can have long wait times. Consider using an authorized private MVD provider for faster service, though they charge an additional service fee ($15 to $40 depending on the provider).

**Not understanding the MVET basis.** The excise tax is based on the purchase price of the vehicle, not the value of the completed build. If you are asked for the value of the conversion, that figure is separate from the MVET calculation.

**Assuming emissions testing is required statewide.** Only Bernalillo County requires emissions testing, and motorhomes are exempt even there.
